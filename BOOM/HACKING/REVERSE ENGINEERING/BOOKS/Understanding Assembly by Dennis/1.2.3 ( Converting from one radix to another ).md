
Conversion relies on **positional notation**, where each digit's weight is determined by its place in the number.

- **Calculation Examples**:
    - In decimal, **1234** is calculated as (103⋅1)+(102⋅2)+(101⋅3)+(100⋅4).
    - In binary, **0b101011** is (25⋅1)+(24⋅0)+(23⋅1)+(22⋅0)+(21⋅1)+(20⋅1), which equals 43 in decimal.
- **Hex-Binary Relationship**: One hexadecimal digit corresponds exactly to **four binary digits** (bits), making it easy to convert between them mentally (e.g., 0xF = 0b1111).
- **Divisibility Shortcuts**: In any base, a number ending in zero is divisible by that base. For example, a hexadecimal address ending in `000` (like `0x41000`) is divisible by 0x1000 (4096), which is often used to identify memory padding or boundaries in file formats like **PE**.
- **Multi-Precision Arithmetic**: When storing extremely large numbers (like RSA keys), they are often split into bytes or 32-bit words, effectively treating the system as having a radix of 256 or 232.
- **Pronunciation and Floating Point**: To avoid confusion with decimal, non-decimal numbers are usually pronounced **digit by digit** (e.g., "one-zero-zero-one"). **Floating point numbers** are typically written with a `.0` at the end (e.g., `123.0`) to distinguish them from integers.
